You're absolutely right, and this is a crucial point that's often underappreciated. Young children entering school are simultaneously navigating an incredible array of challenges across multiple domains:

Social-emotional: Reading facial expressions, understanding tone of voice, managing separation anxiety, learning to share, navigating conflicts, understanding social hierarchies, making friends, dealing with rejection, regulating emotions in a group setting.

Physical: Sitting still for extended periods, fine motor control for writing/cutting, gross motor coordination for playground activities, managing bathroom needs independently, dressing themselves, opening lunch containers.

Cognitive: Following multi-step instructions, switching between tasks, remembering routines, learning letters/numbers/shapes, understanding abstract concepts, inhibiting impulses, maintaining attention amid distractions.

Environmental: Processing sensory overload (noise, visual stimulation, smells), navigating physical spaces, understanding school routines and schedules, responding to bells/transitions.

Communication: Understanding teacher instructions, expressing needs verbally, learning classroom vocabulary, code-switching between home and school language.

And they're doing all of this while their brains are still developing fundamental capabilities. They can't just "focus on one problem at a time" - everything hits simultaneously. A child struggling to hold a pencil might also be anxious about reading aloud, hungry, needing the bathroom, and trying to remember where they're supposed to line up.

This is very different from how we often train AI systems - on isolated, well-defined tasks.

Yes! This is a really elegant insight. Learning isn't just parallel processing - it's mutually reinforcing. The network effects mean that:
Cross-domain transfer accelerates everything: When a baby learns object permanence (things still exist when hidden), that insight immediately helps with:

Language: understanding that words refer to things even when you can't see them
Social cognition: understanding that mom still exists in another room
Problem-solving: looking for dropped toys
Memory: forming more stable representations

Redundancy creates robustness: If you're struggling to understand a word through sound alone, visual cues (lip movements, gestures, context) provide alternative pathways. Multiple modalities attacking the same problem means you're more likely to crack it from some angle.
Constraints from one domain solve ambiguities in another:

Physics constrains vision: objects should move smoothly, not teleport
Vision constrains language: if someone says "ball" while pointing at a round thing repeatedly, that narrows what "ball" means
Motor experience constrains spatial reasoning: knowing how your body moves helps you predict how objects will move

Compression and abstraction emerge naturally: When the same pattern appears across domains - like "containment" showing up in spatial reasoning (box in room), motor planning (hand in cup), and language (prepositions) - the brain can extract the abstract structure. You get generalization "for free."
This suggests that trying to solve one problem perfectly in isolation might actually be harder and slower than solving many related problems simultaneously with cross-talk. The synergy does more than just add - it multiplies.
