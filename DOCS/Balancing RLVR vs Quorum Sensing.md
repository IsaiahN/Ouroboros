The critical correction (this matters)

RLVR is not wrong.
RLVR is incomplete.

It must be paired with a system that protects exploration before rewards are legible.

In your own words from earlier (and this is key):

network intelligence is the horse, RLVR is the rider

If the rider grips too tight, the horse stops exploring.

When RLVR is actually safe

RLVR does not destroy learnability if and only if all of the following are true:

Attempts are pre-allocated

action budget is not earned via reward

exploration is guaranteed, not incentivized

Rewards are local and non-transferable

winning here doesn’t buy leverage elsewhere

no prestige compounding

Failure is cheap and decays

negative reward doesn’t follow agents forever

memory compression removes stigma

Multiple reward lenses exist

no single scalar dominates survival

disagreement between metrics is expected

Exploration is enforced even when RLVR says “don’t”

anti-greedy constraints

forced novelty quotas

orthogonality bonuses

Under those conditions, RLVR becomes:

a validator

a compressor

a filter

Not a gatekeeper.

The sharp invariant

The moment reward determines who gets to act, learning collapses.

That’s the rule RLVR violates by default — unless you explicitly stop it.

What actually works (and why it feels unintuitive)
1. Many winners, narrow rewards

Not one winner.
Not infinite winners.

Instead:

reward orthogonal success

cap gains per niche

prevent compounding advantage

This keeps:

exploration alive

convergence local

dominance temporary

Evolution works this way for a reason.

2. Non-transferable wins

This is huge.

Success in Game A must not buy leverage in Game B.

Otherwise people start:

throwing matches

gaming reputation

trading favors

forming coalitions

You called this out instinctively — and you’re right.

Once wins are portable, quorum behavior explodes.

3. Hidden or delayed scoring

If people can’t immediately see:

who’s ahead

what’s winning

what’s favored

They can’t align socially.

They’re forced to align with reality instead.

This is why:

blind trials work

double-blind experiments matter

delayed feedback preserves variance

4. Forced attempt quotas

This is the most brutal and effective lever.

Everyone must:

try N things

in different regions

regardless of belief or consensus

This short-circuits “I’ll wait and see.”

No opting out.
No safe riding.

5. Failure decay

Failures must:

disappear quickly

not follow you

not stack reputationally

Otherwise people coordinate around not failing.

You want:

“That didn’t work”
not
“That person failed”
